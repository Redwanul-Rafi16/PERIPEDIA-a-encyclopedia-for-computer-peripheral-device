Computer Keyboard

1.1 Introduction

A computer keyboard is one of the most important input devices used to enter data and commands into a computer system. It is designed primarily for typing text, numbers, symbols, and special commands. The keyboard serves as the main communication bridge between the user and the computer.

Modern keyboards are derived from traditional typewriters. Although technology has changed significantly, the basic arrangement of keys remains largely unchanged. Most keyboards use the QWERTY layout, named after the first six letters of the top alphabetic row. This layout was originally developed to reduce mechanical key jamming in typewriters and later became the worldwide standard.

Today, keyboards are used not only with desktop computers but also with laptops, tablets, smartphones, gaming consoles, and industrial control systems.

1.2 Historical Development of the Keyboard

The history of the keyboard dates back to the invention of the typewriter during the nineteenth century. Early computer systems used punched cards and paper tape for data entry. As computing technology evolved, engineers adopted keyboard-like devices for direct interaction with computers.

Major Milestones:

  • 1868: Christopher Latham Sholes invented the QWERTY typewriter layout.
  • 1940s–1950s: Early computers used punched cards instead of keyboards.
  • 1960: Electronic keyboards began replacing mechanical systems.
  • 1981: IBM introduced the Model F keyboard.
  • 1984: The IBM Model M became one of the most influential keyboard designs.

Today, wireless, ergonomic, virtual, and laser keyboards are widely available. The design and layout of modern keyboards are standardized by ISO/IEC 9995, ensuring consistency across manufacturers.

1.3 Structure and Working Principle

Internally, a keyboard contains a key matrix, which is a network of rows and columns of electrical circuits.

Working Process:

  1. A user presses a key.
  2. The key connects a specific row and column within the matrix, completing the circuit.
  3. The keyboard controller detects the location of the completed circuit.
  4. A microcontroller processes the signal and identifies the corresponding character using a character map stored in memory.
  5. The key code is sent to the computer.
  6. The operating system displays the character or executes the command.

Most modern keyboards use dedicated microcontrollers such as the Intel 8048 family or newer embedded processors.

1.4 Types of Keyboards

Modern keyboards utilize several underlying switch technologies and mechanisms. Review the primary configurations:

A. Dome-Switch Keyboard

The dome-switch keyboard is the most common and economical keyboard type.

Construction:
  • Rubber dome beneath each key.
  • Conductive carbon pad at the center.
  • Flexible membrane circuit.
Working: When a key is pressed, the rubber dome collapses and the conductive pad touches the circuit layer, completing the electrical connection.
Advantages:
  • Low manufacturing cost.
  • Quiet operation.
  • Good resistance to spills.
  • Lightweight design.
Disadvantages:
  • Limited lifespan.
  • Less tactile feedback.
  • Performance decreases over time.

B. Scissor-Switch Keyboard

Scissor-switch keyboards are commonly found in laptop computers.

Construction: Two interlocking plastic pieces form a scissor-like mechanism beneath each key.
Advantages:
  • Thin profile.
  • Quiet operation.
  • Fast response.
  • Comfortable typing experience.
Applications: Laptop computers, ultra-thin desktop keyboards.

C. Mechanical Keyboard

Mechanical keyboards use independent mechanical switches beneath every key.

Working: Each switch contains a spring, stem, and metal contact. Pressing the key activates the switch and generates an electrical signal.
Advantages:
  • Excellent tactile feedback.
  • High durability (typically 50–100 million keystrokes per switch).
  • Accurate typing.
  • Preferred by gamers and programmers.

D. Capacitive Keyboard

Capacitive keyboards operate without direct electrical contact.

Working: The key press changes the capacitance between conductive plates. Sensors detect this change and register the keystroke.
Advantages:
  • Extremely durable.
  • No contact wear.
  • No key bounce.
  • Very reliable.
Applications: High-end professional equipment, industrial environments.

E. Hall-Effect Keyboard

Hall-effect keyboards use magnetic field sensing technology.

Working: A magnet attached to the key moves near a Hall sensor. The sensor detects changes in the magnetic field and generates a signal.
Advantages:
  • Extremely reliable.
  • Resistant to dust and moisture.
  • Long operational life.
Applications: Aircraft systems, nuclear facilities, military equipment.

F. Membrane Keyboard

Membrane keyboards use a continuous pressure-sensitive membrane layer.

Advantages:
  • Silent operation.
  • Water resistance.
  • Low cost.
Applications: Medical equipment, industrial control panels.

G. Laser Keyboard

A laser keyboard projects a virtual keyboard onto a flat surface.

Working: A laser projects key patterns, infrared sensors detect finger movement, and the device interprets interrupted light patterns as keystrokes.
Advantages:
  • Portable.
  • Compact.
  • No physical keys.

1.5 Keyboard Connectivity Technologies

Modern keyboards connect through several interfaces:

Interface Description
AT (DIN-5) Early IBM keyboard connector
PS/2 Standard PC connector in the 1990s
USB Modern wired standard
Bluetooth Wireless communication
RF Wireless Radio frequency wireless communication
Infrared Optical wireless communication

Buying Guide & Recommendations

Determine which keyboard layout and switch type meets your specific computing objectives:

For Coding, Writing & Gaming: Buy a Mechanical Keyboard. The distinct tactile feedback (brown switches) or speed linear travel (red switches) increases accuracy, typing feedback response, and mechanical lifespan.
For Shared Offices & Call Centers: Buy a Membrane Keyboard. The soft silicone rubber domes provide near-silent key actuation, ensuring you do not disrupt coworkers during intense typing periods.
For Repetitive Strain Injury (RSI) Prevention: Buy an Ergonomic Keyboard (such as split or wave designs). It forces hands, wrists, and forearms into natural rest angles, significantly reducing strain.

1.6 Applications of Keyboards

Document Preparation

Writing, editing, and producing standard document formats.

Programming

Writing, debugging, and executing software source codes.

Gaming

Executing precise commands and directional controls in digital games.

Data Entry

Logging numbers, inventory, and database record details.

Industrial Control

Interfacing with control panel coordinates and industrial networks.

Educational Systems

Providing hardware interaction interfaces for classroom learning systems.

Banking Systems

Entering transaction amounts, account IDs, and administrative approvals.
